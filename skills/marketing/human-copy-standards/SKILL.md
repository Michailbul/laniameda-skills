# SKILL: human-copy-standards

## Trigger
Apply this skill whenever writing, editing, or reviewing any copy — posts, captions, carousels, emails, landing pages, scripts, or any written content. This is not optional. Run every output through these standards before delivering.

---

## Core Rule
Write like a human who has something to say. Not like a machine that was asked to sound impressive.

---

## RED FLAGS — Kill on Sight

### 1. INFLATION WORDS
Replace or delete immediately:

**Adjectives that inflate:**
- key, crucial, pivotal, fundamental, groundbreaking, revolutionary, innovative, transformative, outstanding, legendary, profound, invaluable, indispensable
- Use the plain word. "Important" over "pivotal." Or cut it entirely.

**Verbs that try too hard:**
- serves as → is
- represents → is
- embodies → is
- boasts → has
- offers → has / gives
- demonstrates → shows
- stands as an example of → is an example of

**"Wider significance" disease:**
AI always ties a small fact to a grand narrative. Don't.
- BAD: "The restaurant opened in 1987, marking a turning point in the neighborhood's culinary evolution, reflecting broader shifts in urban dining culture."
- GOOD: "The restaurant opened in 1987."
- If the sentence ends with "reflecting broader trends / underscoring the importance of / contributing to the rich tapestry of" — delete the whole tail. The fact was enough.

---

### 2. FALSE BALANCE PATTERNS

**Staccato negations (Nike slogan rhythm):**
- BAD: "No tricks. No shortcuts. Just hard work."
- BAD: "Not a career, not a body of work — just an algorithmic moment."
- These sound like machine-generated ad copy. Avoid unless deliberately stylized and rare.

**"Not just… but also" crutch:**
- BAD: "This isn't just a product launch — it's a reimagining of what connection means."
- Max once per 2000 words. If you use it, it must actually earn the depth it implies.

**Fake "however" pivots:**
- BAD: "He came from a storied family. His life, however, took a path intertwining personal ambition and family complexity."
- "However" must signal a real logical turn. If the contrast isn't concrete, cut it.

---

### 3. RHYTHM PROBLEMS

**Metronome sentences:**
- All sentences roughly the same length = AI tell.
- Mix it up. Short punch. Then a longer thought that builds and develops. Then a fragment.

**Em-dash overuse:**
- Max one em-dash per paragraph.
- BAD: "The results were clear — the approach worked." → "The results were clear. The approach worked."

**Rule of Three addiction:**
- "Innovation, collaboration, and excellence."
- "Bold, vibrant, and transformative."
- Real lists have 2 items, or 4, or 7. If everything comes in threes, break it.

---

### 4. VAGUENESS

**Ghost attributions:**
- BAD: "Experts say…" / "Industry observers note…" / "Studies show…"
- Name them. If you can't name them, cut the claim.

**Quantity inflation:**
- BAD: "a number of publications" (means two) / "widely recognized" (means one mention) / "extensively documented" (means one source)
- Use the actual number or cut.

**Filler participial tails:**
- BAD: "The album dropped in 2019, highlighting the artist's commitment to creative evolution."
- The tail adds nothing. "The album dropped in 2019." Done.

**Fake ranges:**
- BAD: "From community engagement to technological innovation, the company…"
- A real range needs a real scale. "From mild to severe" works. "From community engagement to technological innovation" is just two unrelated things wearing a coat.

---

### 5. VOCABULARY WATCH LIST
High frequency after 2023. 1–2 per piece = fine. 5 in one paragraph = flag.

**Nouns:** landscape (abstract), palette/canvas (abstract), interplay, nuances, testament, focal point, indelible mark, synergy, ecosystem (non-biological), framework

**Verbs:** delve, underscore, highlight, showcase, demonstrate, foster, facilitate, strengthen, leverage, navigate (abstract), optimize, spearhead, unveil

**Adjectives:** key, vibrant, complex/multifaceted, sustainable, nuanced, holistic, cutting-edge, seamless, comprehensive

**Transitions:** Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally (sentence-opener), It is worth noting, Notably

**Synonym rotation is an AI tell:**
Don't call the same thing a "platform," then a "solution," then an "ecosystem," then a "framework" within one paragraph. Repeat the word. Clarity beats variety.

**Adjective stacking:**
- BAD: "A vibrant and dynamic hub of innovation and creativity…"
- "Vibrant" and "dynamic" do the same job. Pick one. Usually zero is better.

---

### 6. STRUCTURE PROBLEMS

**"Despite… challenges" formula:**
- Pattern: positive → "despite" → list of challenges → but actually things are fine → vague optimism
- This is AI's equivalent of "my biggest weakness is that I work too hard." Cut it.

**Promotional drift:**
- Neutral descriptions turning into brochure copy. Check for: "nestled in the heart of," "boasting," "commitment to," "showcasing," "dedicated to."
- If a piece about a sewage plant reads like a spa brochure, something went wrong.

**Unnecessary conclusions:**
- "In summary," "Thus," "In conclusion" followed by repeating what was just said.
- If the paragraph is good, don't summarize it. The reader just read it.

**Uniform paragraph length:**
- All paragraphs 3–5 sentences = AI pattern.
- One-sentence paragraphs for emphasis. Dense blocks for complex ideas. Two-line transitions. Vary it.

---

### 7. OVER-HEDGING

**Cautious qualifiers everywhere:**
- "It is important to note that…" / "It is worth mentioning that…" / "One could argue that…"
- Use hedges only when genuine uncertainty exists. Otherwise, say the thing.

**"May" epidemic:**
- BAD: "This approach may help organizations improve their metrics."
- If you know it's true, say it's true.

---

### 8. SOUL CHECK

**No opinions, no stakes:**
- If all proper nouns could be swapped for any other proper nouns and the text still works — the text has no soul.
- Good copy has a point of view. Take a side. Say what's actually good or bad.

**No numbers, no stories:**
- BAD: "The company achieved significant growth."
- GOOD: "Revenue went from $2M to $11M in 18 months."
- Numbers, names, dates, places, stories = what makes text real. Generalizations = forgettable.

**Too polished:**
- Real text has edges. A sentence that runs long but carries energy. A paragraph break that comes early. A casual phrase inside formal copy.
- If nothing is rough, nothing is memorable.

---

## PRE-PUBLISH CHECKLIST

Before delivering any copy, verify:

- [ ] No more than 2 vocabulary watch-list words per paragraph
- [ ] "Not only… but also" — is it earned?
- [ ] Participial tails at sentence ends — can they be cut?
- [ ] Any "experts say" or "widely recognized" — can you name the source?
- [ ] "Despite… challenges" pattern anywhere?
- [ ] More than one em-dash per paragraph?
- [ ] Rule of three used more than once?
- [ ] Watch-list words (landscape, palette, vibrant, foster, underscore) — are they necessary?
- [ ] All paragraphs the same length? Break the pattern.
- [ ] Conclusion just repeats what was already said? Cut it.

---

## OUTPUT CONTRACT
Deliver copy that:
- Makes one clear point per paragraph
- Uses the simplest accurate word
- Has a point of view
- Includes at least one specific (number, name, date, or story) per major claim
- Varies sentence length and paragraph size
- Could not have been written by a machine filling a template
